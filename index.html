Olympic-Level Execution
TECHNIQUE LIBRARY

Complete breakdowns of every stroke, phase, drill, and race element — with elite benchmarks, exact numbers, and the physiology behind every movement.

ENTRY & HIGH ELBOW CATCH

▾

Hand enters narrow (inside shoulder line) at 10–15° downward. The high elbow catch is the single highest-ROI skill in swimming. Elbow rises and flares outward while wrist presses down, creating a large vertical forearm surface against still water.

🎯 Cue: "Reach around a barrel." Elbow sets high and wide before the hand moves back at all.
Entry angle

10–15° below horizontal.

Elbow height

Always above wrist through catch.

Catch depth

Wrist vertical at 30–40cm depth.

Dressel benchmark

Entry to catch: 0.18–0.22 seconds.

PULL PATH & HAND ACCELERATION

▾

The pull follows a slight S-curve. Hand accelerates throughout — slowest at catch, fastest at exit. The hand must push all the way past the hip — thumb brushing the thigh.

🎯 Cue: "Slow in, fast out." The catch should feel controlled. The finish should feel like a snap.

BODY ROTATION & HEAD POSITION

▾

Sprint rotation: 35–45°. Distance: 25–35°. Head position: hairline at the surface, eyes at the pool floor. Even a 2-inch head lift forces the hips down.

6-BEAT KICK MECHANICS

▾

Elite sprinters derive 30–40% of propulsion from the kick. The kick originates at the hip — knee bends only 30–40° on the downbeat. Ankle plantarflexion is the biggest limiter for most swimmers.

🎯 Cue: "Kick from your hip pocket, not your knee."

GOLF SCORE SYSTEM

▾

Golf Score = 50m split (seconds) + stroke count per 50m. Lower is always better. Elite benchmarks: Below 55 = world class. 55–62 = senior national. 62–70 = competitive club.

BODY POSITION & DUCK DRILL

▾

Correct position: hips at the surface, ears slightly submerged, eyes toward feet. Even a small hip drop creates exponentially increasing drag.

🎯 Cue: "Squeeze a coin between your glutes, belly button to the ceiling."

PINKY ENTRY & 2-2-2 DRILL

▾

Entry is pinky-first, hand rotated so thumb points inward toward centerline. Arm enters directly above the shoulder. The 2-2-2 drill (2 right strokes, 2 left strokes, 2 full) isolates each side.

2K1 SCULL — THE CORE DRILL

▾

2k1 (two kicks, one stroke) is the foundational breaststroke drill. Doubling the kick slows tempo dramatically — forcing the swimmer to feel the glide, catch, and full out-scull before pulling.

🎯 Progression: Deep 2k1 scull → Shallow 2k1 scull → Flat rhythm → Full speed.

BREASTSTROKE KICK — 60–70% OF PROPULSION

▾

Heels draw up narrow — not wide. Feet evert into "duck feet" before sweeping in a circular arc and finishing together in streamline.

🎯 Cue: "Heels narrow, toes wide, sweep out-around-together."

UNDULATION — THE WAVE FROM CHEST TO FEET

▾

Butterfly is wave-driven, not arm-driven. The movement: chest presses DOWN → hips rise → feet drive DOWN. Hip amplitude: 20–30cm.

🎯 Cue: "Chest DOWN first — everything else follows."

2-2-2 DRILL & ONE-ARM FLY

▾

2-2-2 drill: 2 right-arm strokes, 2 left-arm strokes, 2 full butterfly strokes. Isolates each side's pull pattern and makes bilateral asymmetries obvious immediately.

UNDERWATER DOLPHIN KICK — THE 5TH STROKE

▾

Underwater dolphin kick is the fastest human movement through water. At elite level, UW velocity exceeds surface swimming speed. The program features UW 15m on nearly every session.

🎯 Every rep is a race-simulation. Count kicks per 15m weekly.
Streamline

Hands stacked dominant-on-top, arms past ears, core rigid.

Kick depth

25–35cm amplitude. Hip-driven.

Swim depth

0.5–0.6m below surface.

Elite rate

180–210 kicks/min.

FREESTYLE FLIP TURN

▾

Elite push-off velocities reach 7.5+ m/s. Every 0.1s lost at the wall costs far more metabolically to recover. Approach at full speed. Initiate flip 1.2–1.5 body lengths from wall. Drive into tight streamline with ≥5m UW dolphin kick off every wall.

BREASTSTROKE PULLOUT

▾

The breaststroke pullout is the fastest point in the stroke. Legal sequence: push off → streamline glide → one full underwater pull → one dolphin kick → breakout. Begin the pull the moment you sense velocity starting to drop.

GRAB START & TRACK START MECHANICS

▾

Track start (one foot back) is now dominant at elite level. Reaction time target: <0.65 seconds. Takeoff angle: 35–45°.

Reaction time

Elite target: 0.60–0.64s.

Takeoff angle

35–45° for maximum horizontal distance.

Entry angle

15–20° nose-down at water entry.

UW distance

Elite: 10–15m before breakout.

IM ORDER & TRANSITION STRATEGY

▾

IM order: Butterfly → Backstroke → Breaststroke → Freestyle. Each transition is a critical moment where drag and tempo change dramatically.

Fly pacing

Go out controlled — 200 IM is not a 50 fly sprint.

Breast leg

The race decider. If you've gone out too hard, breaststroke is where you pay.

Elite split

Fly 27%, back 26%, breast 27%, free 20%.

Between Events

INTRA-MEET

  • 30–60g simple carbs every 60–90 min
  • 250–350ml water every 30–45 min

THE 30-MINUTE RECOVERY WINDOW

Glycogen resynthesis rate is highest in the first 30–45 minutes after training. Target: 1–1.2 g/kg carbs + 0.3–0.4 g/kg protein. Best options: chocolate milk, rice + protein, yogurt + banana.

Even 2% dehydration reduces performance by 10–20%. Swimmers underestimate fluid loss — you sweat just as much in the pool.

DAILY HYDRATION

  • Baseline: 35–45ml per kg bodyweight
  • Add 400–600ml per hour of training
  • Urine target: pale yellow
  • Electrolytes mandatory on 90+ min sessions

Compete at Your Best
RACE STRATEGY

MEET DAY TIMELINE

  • T-3 hrs: Pre-meet meal. Carb-heavy, familiar, low fiber/fat.
  • T-75 min: Begin warm-up. Never skip.
  • T-30 min: Complete warm-up. Dry off, stay warm, hydrate.
  • T-10 min: Behind the blocks. Controlled breathing.

BEHIND THE BLOCKS

  • Focus only on what you control: first 5 strokes, your turn, your pace
  • Box breathing: 4 in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold
  • One technical focus point only — more than one is too many
  • Never watch your main rivals warm up

COMPETITION WARM-UP PROTOCOL

  • 400–600m easy: HR below 22. No rushing.
  • 4×50 kick: Fins, easy to moderate.
  • 4×50 drills: Race-stroke drills. One technical cue.
  • 4×25 builds: Gradually build to 85% on final 25.
  • 2×15m sprint: Full race pace from a push.
  • 200m easy cool-down: Drop HR before the race.
Total warm-up: 1,200–1,800m max. More than that is a training set before your race.

TAPER & RECOVERY

SPRINT TAPER (7–14 days)

  • Week 2 before: volume drops 30–40%, intensity maintained
  • Week 1 before: volume drops another 40%, race-pace work daily
  • Day before: 20–30 min easy + 4–6×25 race-pace
  • Avoid long rest — 7+ days off makes sprinters feel slow

DISTANCE TAPER (14–21 days)

  • Week 3 before: volume down 20%, quality maintained
  • Week 2 before: volume down 40%
  • Week 1 before: volume down 60–70%
  • Trust the process — feeling sluggish in week 2 is normal
The Taper "Flat" is Normal. Most swimmers feel terrible mid-taper. This almost always resolves by race day. Do NOT extend training because taper feels bad.

The Psychological Edge
MENTAL PERFORMANCE

VISUALIZATION — MENTAL REHEARSAL

Visualization activates 60–70% of the same motor cortex activity as physical practice. 10–15 min daily — morning after waking or evening before sleep. Full race visualization should match real race time.

  • Both first-person AND third-person perspectives
  • Include sensory detail: chlorine, water feel, starter signal
  • Visualize the process more than the outcome
  • Also visualize imperfect scenarios and recovery

OPTIMAL AROUSAL

Too calm = under-activated. Too anxious = overthinking. Every swimmer has their own optimal zone — finding it is a training task.

REGULATION TOOLS

DOWN-regulation: Box breathing 5×, progressive muscle relaxation, slow warm-up, 60–80 BPM music.

UP-regulation: Explosive warm-up bursts, 120–140 BPM music, power posing 2 min, activating self-talk.

FOCUS CUES

  • Maximum 2 focus cues per race
  • Cues should be action-based: "High elbow" not "Don't drop my elbow"
  • Practice cues in training until automatic
  • Self-talk in final 50m: "This is where you train for."

PRE-RACE ROUTINE

  • T-15 min: 3 min box breathing
  • T-12 min: Visualize first 50m in real time
  • T-8 min: Review 2 focus cues only
  • T-5 min: Physical activation — arm swings, fast squats
  • T-1 min: On blocks. One breath. Activate.

Stay in the Water
INJURY PREVENTION
Swimmer's Shoulder is the most common swimming injury. 60–80% of elite swimmers experience it. Most cases are preventable.

CAUSES

  • Serratus anterior weakness
  • Rotator cuff imbalance
  • Crossover entry (crossing centerline)
  • Flat, dropped elbow catch
  • Excessive paddle volume

PREVENTION

  • Serratus punches: 3×15 daily
  • External rotation: sidelying, 3×15, 3×/week
  • Correct entry: never cross centerline
  • High elbow catch on every single stroke

BREASTSTROKER'S KNEE

  • Primary cause: hip adductor weakness
  • Copenhagen planks 3×/week
  • Narrow heel recovery drill
  • Self-test: kick on your back — can you see the inside of your knees?

LOW BACK PAIN

  • Cause: tight hip flexors + weak glutes + weak deep core
  • Daily hip flexor stretching (90/90, couch stretch)
  • Glute activation: hip thrusts, clamshells
  • Dead bug for deep core stability
Hip impingement and flexor tightness show up most in breaststroke and butterfly swimmers, where the hip moves through a large range under load every single cycle.

CAUSES

  • Repetitive breaststroke whip-kick external rotation under load
  • Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) from years of forced hip ROM
  • Chronically tight hip flexors from the prone swimming position
  • Weak glute medius — lets the hip drop or rotate during the kick
  • Butterfly's repeated hip-driven undulation without adequate mobility work

PREVENTION

  • 90/90 hip switches daily — builds rotational range in both directions
  • Glute medius activation: banded clamshells, monster walks, 3×/week
  • Couch stretch for hip flexor length, 2 min per side daily
  • Limit breaststroke whip-kick volume during growth spurts in younger swimmers
  • Any pinching pain at the front of the hip during kick — stop and get it assessed, don't swim through it

RETURN TO KICK AFTER HIP PAIN

  • Phase 1: Flutter or dolphin kick only — no breaststroke whip-kick
  • Phase 2: Slow, shallow-range breaststroke kick on the back, focused on pain-free range only
  • Phase 3: Full kick volume, reintroduced at 50% of normal load for the first week back

SWIMMER'S ELBOW (MEDIAL/LATERAL EPICONDYLITIS)

Less common than shoulder issues but shows up in swimmers who paddle-train heavily or have a flat, dropped-elbow catch that loads the forearm flexors and extensors instead of the lat and pec.

  • Primary cause: excessive paddle volume before the forearm has built tolerance
  • Secondary cause: a catch that relies on wrist/forearm flexion instead of a high elbow — the small muscles around the elbow take over for the big back muscles
  • Prevention: introduce paddles gradually over weeks, not days; fix the high-elbow catch first so the lat does the work
  • Dryland: wrist curls and reverse wrist curls, 3×12, to build tendon tolerance
  • Self-test: pain when gripping or extending the wrist against resistance points to the elbow, not the shoulder

NECK PAIN — BREATHING AND HEAD POSITION

Neck pain in swimmers is almost always a head-position problem, not a neck-strength problem. Habitually lifting the head to breathe instead of rotating it within the body's bow wave puts repetitive strain on the cervical spine across thousands of strokes per week.

  • Freestyle: lifting the head to breathe instead of rotating with the body roll — fix the stroke before treating the symptom
  • Backstroke: Duck-drill imbalance or chronic chin-tuck looking at the feet — keep the neck neutral
  • Breaststroke: lifting the chin to breathe instead of riding the body's natural lift from the pull
  • Prevention: daily neck CARs (controlled articular rotations), and film yourself breathing from above to check for head lift
  • Persistent neck pain unrelated to a recent technique change warrants a look from a physio rather than continued self-treatment
Breathing issues in swimmers are common and often under-discussed. Chlorine exposure, breath-holding training, and the unique mechanics of swim breathing all play a role.

EXERCISE-INDUCED BRONCHOCONSTRICTION (EIB)

  • Significantly more common in competitive swimmers than the general athletic population, likely linked to chronic low-level chlorine byproduct exposure in indoor pools
  • Symptoms: coughing, wheezing, or chest tightness during or after hard sets — easy to mistake for "just being out of breath"
  • If symptoms appear consistently with intensity (not just once), get evaluated by a doctor rather than assuming it's normal fatigue
  • Good pool ventilation and showering promptly after practice reduce chlorine byproduct exposure

UNDERWATER WORK & BREATH-HOLDING SAFETY

  • Shallow water blackout is a real risk from repeated breath-holding or hyperventilation before underwater swimming — it can happen with no warning symptoms
  • Never practice extended underwater distance alone, and never hyperventilate before a long underwater swim to "load up on oxygen"
  • UW training sets (15m off the wall) at normal training volumes are not the same risk category as deliberate breath-hold/static apnea training — the latter needs supervision and should not be self-coached from generic advice
  • Coaches should know the difference between healthy UW kick technique work and breath-hold training that carries real risk

GENERAL RESPIRATORY HEALTH FOR SWIMMERS

  • A persistent cough, recurring chest tightness, or breathing that feels disproportionately hard relative to your fitness level are reasons to see a doctor, not push through
  • Asthma is manageable for competitive swimmers with proper diagnosis and treatment — many elite swimmers compete successfully with diagnosed asthma
